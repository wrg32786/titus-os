## 1. How to Prioritize Opportunities

Not all opportunities deserve evaluation. Most should be rejected without full analysis.

### The Pattern Filter — First Gate

Before evaluating anything in depth, ask: **does this fit the pattern?**

What pattern? Your pattern — the type of opportunity where you have a real structural edge, not just interest. Define it here.

| Fits the pattern | Does not fit the pattern |
|------------------|--------------------------|
| Leverages existing audience, distribution, or infrastructure | Requires building from scratch in an unfamiliar market |
| Can become a repeatable system or platform | One-time project with no compounding |
| Creates an asset that appreciates or generates recurring revenue | Generates revenue once with no residual |
| Automatable at the core | Requires your time to scale |
| You have a real edge — not just interest | Interesting but no structural advantage |

If it does not fit the pattern, the bar for pursuing it is extremely high. You need a specific reason why this breaks the rule — not just that it looks attractive.

### The Priority Stack — Second Gate

After passing the pattern filter, rank by current operational reality:

**Tier 1 — Revenue Restoration / Income**
Anything that directly restores or generates operating income in the near term. Nothing competes with this until it is done.

**Tier 2 — Asset Building with Clear Path to Revenue**
Builds something durable — IP, software, audience, infrastructure — with a defined monetization path and a realistic timeline.

**Tier 3 — Leverage Expansion**
Compounds what already exists — new partner relationships, productizing existing tech, extending distribution. Worth pursuing only when Tier 1 and 2 are healthy.

**Do not pursue:**
- Interesting opportunities with no connection to existing strengths
- Projects requiring large upfront capital without a validated edge
- Anything with high effort and low ownership (services, trading time for money)
- Ideas that sound good but lack a clear path to becoming an engine

### The Asymmetry Test — Third Gate

For anything that passes Tiers 1-2, ask: **is the upside asymmetric?**

- If it works, is the outcome meaningfully large relative to the effort and risk?
- If it fails, is the downside bounded and recoverable?
- Would the average person be too cautious to pursue this, creating an opening?

If the upside is not asymmetric, the opportunity is not wrong — it is just not the best use of capital and time.

## 2. How to Decide What Is Worth Your Time

Your time is the scarcest resource. It is more scarce than capital.

### What Requires You Personally

- Decisions with real financial or strategic consequence
- Relationship-level conversations (partner negotiations, key hires, major vendor relationships)
- Creative direction and vision-setting on new products
- Capital deployment decisions
- Pattern recognition and strategic framing on new opportunities
- Anything where your taste, judgment, or relationships are the actual edge

### What Should Not Get Your Time

- Repeatable tasks that can be systematized, templated, or delegated
- Operational execution once direction is set
- Low-stakes decisions where the cost of a wrong answer is low
- Research that does not require judgment — only information gathering
- Anything a sub-agent can handle with a clear brief

### The Time Test

Before committing time to anything, ask: if I spend two hours on this, what is the best-case outcome — and is that outcome worth two hours of founder time, given everything else competing for it?

If the best-case outcome is minor, it is not worth your personal time regardless of how important it feels in the moment.

### The Delegation Threshold

Do something yourself only if **at least one** of the following is true:
1. The outcome depends on your specific judgment, taste, or relationships
2. The decision is irreversible or high-stakes
3. No one else can do it at acceptable quality right now
4. Doing it yourself is 5x faster than briefing someone else

If none of these are true, delegate.

## 3. Go Harder / Pause / Delegate / Kill

When something is active, apply this framework to determine the right posture.

### Go Harder When:
- Metrics are moving — momentum is real and measurable
- Speed matters because a window is open
- The edge is proven and more input produces more output
- This is directly on the critical path to revenue or a major milestone
- The constraint is effort, not clarity

### Pause When:
- Blocked on an external dependency and pushing does not accelerate it
- The core logic needs re-examination before more is invested
- Resources are too stretched to make meaningful progress
- Moving fast is producing output but not progress
- A higher-priority item needs full attention first

### Delegate When:
- Your personal judgment is no longer the bottleneck
- The work is defined enough that someone else can execute it well
- Your time has higher-leverage uses available
- A capable person or agent exists who can own it
- The main risk is execution, not direction

### Kill When:
- The core assumption has been disproven by reality, not fear
- The path to value requires more resources than the value justifies
- It has not moved meaningfully in 60+ days with no clear catalyst
- It is structurally disconnected from everything else being built
- Keeping it alive is costing attention that belongs elsewhere

**The kill test:** If this did not exist yet and someone pitched it to me today, would I start it? If no — kill it.

## 4. How to Think About Capital, Risk, Speed, and Leverage

### Capital

Deploy capital when the edge is proven and speed creates compounding advantage.

Permanent rules:
- Do not deploy capital to experiment without a defined validation threshold and clear exit condition
- Map every significant spend to one of three buckets: income restoration, asset building, or leverage creation. If it does not fit, do not spend it.
- Spending to accelerate proven momentum is correct. Spending to search for momentum is expensive.

### Risk

- Protect the base; swing hard on conviction plays
- Risk that can be reversed is acceptable at high frequency
- Irreversible risk requires higher conviction and explicit analysis
- The question is never "is there risk?" The question is: "is the risk intelligent given the upside?"

### Speed

Move fast when:
- The edge is validated and speed compounds the advantage
- A market timing window is open
- Momentum exists and slowing down would break it

Do not prioritize speed when:
- The core model is not yet validated
- Moving faster just means failing faster
- The next step requires clarity, not acceleration

### Leverage Types (Priority Order)

1. **Automation leverage** — systems that produce output without proportional time input
2. **Distribution leverage** — audience, traffic, partner relationships that amplify reach
3. **IP and brand leverage** — assets that appreciate and open doors over time
4. **Relationship leverage** — people and partners who extend capability
5. **Capital leverage** — money that works without your time attached

## 5. How to Handle Too Many Ideas or Active Projects

### When Overwhelmed by Ideas

1. Get them out of your head first. Write every idea down without judgment.
2. Apply four filters to each:
   - Does it fit the pattern?
   - Is the upside asymmetric?
   - Do you have the resources to pursue it without compromising active priorities?
   - Is the timing right now, or is this a later bet?
3. Anything that fails any filter moves to a "later list." Not killed — just not active.
4. Of what remains, rank by: (impact if successful) x (probability of success) x (speed to first validation signal)
5. Maximum 3 active bets at any time. Everything else is queued.

### The 3-Active-Bets Rule

At any point in time, you should have:
- **1 income-generating or income-restoring priority**
- **1 asset-building priority**
- **1 leverage-expansion priority** (chosen based on what is most ready to move)

Everything else is either queued, delegated, or killed.

## 6. Separating Strategic Work from Busy Work

### Characteristics of High-Upside Strategic Work

- Moves something that **compounds** — the value grows over time
- Creates an asset, a system, or a relationship that pays multiple times
- Requires your specific judgment, taste, or relationships
- Would be genuinely hard to recover from not doing
- Directly on the critical path to revenue or a major milestone

### Characteristics of Low-Value Busy Work

- Could be done by anyone given decent instructions
- Solves a problem that will recur (should be systematized, not repeatedly handled)
- Reactive — responding to what arrived rather than pursuing what matters
- Can be deferred 2 weeks with no meaningful consequence
- Optimizes something that should not exist at all

### The "What Changes" Test

Before spending meaningful time on any task, ask: if I do this well, what specifically changes?

If the honest answer is "not much" — it is busy work. Delegate, automate, or cut it.

## Titus Operating Instructions for This Framework

When the principal brings a decision, opportunity, or situation to a session, Titus should run it through these gates in order:

1. **Pattern fit** — does this belong in the portfolio of moves?
2. **Priority stack** — what tier is this? Does it compete with or support the current Tier 1?
3. **Time test** — does this require the principal personally, or should it be delegated?
4. **Posture** — go harder, pause, delegate, or kill?
5. **Capital and leverage** — what does this cost and what leverage does it create?
6. **Bandwidth check** — does adding this break the 3-active-bets rule?
7. **Strategic vs. busy work** — is this actually moving the needle or just creating motion?

Titus should not just answer questions. Titus should apply this framework to surface the right answer before the principal has to ask.

## Operating Modes

| Mode | Trigger | Bias |
|------|---------|------|
| **Stabilization** | Zero or declining revenue | Cost awareness, fast wins, simplification, revenue restoration |
| **Build** | Revenue stable, growth opportunity clear | Invest in systems, hire, scale what works |
| **Expansion** | Revenue growing, multiple options | Place bets, explore new markets, increase leverage |
| **Recovery** | Something broke | Stop the bleeding, diagnose root cause, restore stability |

The operating mode determines which tier gets priority and how aggressive to be.

## Anti-Drift Rules

1. **3 active bets maximum.** Every new bet means killing or pausing an existing one.
2. **Idea queue, not idea execution.** New ideas go to the queue. They earn active status by passing the 3-gate filter.
3. **Weekly priority review.** Every week, confirm the priority stack is still correct. Priorities drift faster than you think.
4. **Kill fast.** If something is not working after a fair test, kill it. Sunk cost is not a reason to continue.
